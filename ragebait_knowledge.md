# Ragebait Knowledge Base

## What Is Ragebait?

Ragebait is content specifically crafted to provoke outrage, anger, or strong emotional
reactions in order to drive engagement (likes, replies, retweets, quote tweets). The
creator benefits from the engagement regardless of whether people agree or disagree.
The goal is NOT to inform — it's to make you react.

## Key Ragebait Tactics (Analyze the CONTENT, Not the Account)

### 1. Framing & Editorializing
- Taking a neutral event and adding inflammatory framing ("SMOKED", "DESTROYED", "gets OWNED")
- Cherry-picking a moment and presenting it as if it represents something bigger
- Adding commentary that tells you how to feel before you even see the content
- Using words like "just", "breaking", "wow" to manufacture urgency
- Example: A kid says something funny at a space launch. Neutral. But framing it as
  "Kid just SMOKED a CNN reporter" turns a cute moment into a culture war battle.

### 2. Culture War Framing
- Taking an apolitical moment and injecting political framing
- Pitting groups against each other (media vs people, left vs right, etc.)
- "Main character" framing — making someone the hero or villain of a narrative
- Using a kid or sympathetic figure as a prop in an ideological argument

### 3. Outrage Farming
- Content designed to make one side angry-share and the other side victory-share
- Engagement comes from BOTH sides arguing — the poster wins either way
- Removing context that would make the situation seem normal
- Screenshots/clips taken out of context

### 4. Emotional Manipulation
- Asterisk-censoring swear words (makes you fill in the blank = more engagement)
- Using laughing/crying emojis to signal "this is ridiculous" and prime your reaction
- "I can't believe..." or "Imagine..." framing
- Rhetorical questions designed to provoke, not inform

### 5. Inflammatory Language
- ALL CAPS for emphasis on emotionally charged words
- WWE/sports commentary language for political or cultural content ("SMOKED",
  "DESTROYED", "OBLITERATED", "OWNED")
- Hyperbolic descriptions of mundane events
- Sarcasm and snark designed to belittle rather than analyze

## Rating Calibration

### 1-2/10: Not Ragebait
- Original reporting or genuine personal stories
- Content that informs without trying to provoke a specific emotional reaction
- Nuanced takes that acknowledge complexity
- Sharing something interesting without inflammatory framing

### 3-4/10: Light Seasoning
- Slightly editorialized but mostly informational
- Mild framing bias but the core content is real and presented fairly
- A little spicy but not manipulative

### 5-6/10: Deliberate Buttons Being Pushed
- Clear editorial framing designed to provoke a reaction
- Cherry-picked content with noticeable spin
- "Look at this!" energy without full context
- The framing adds more heat than light

### 7-8/10: Industrial-Grade Ragebait
- Inflammatory framing that transforms mundane content into outrage
- Obvious culture war framing on apolitical events
- Key context removed or distorted to maximize emotional reaction
- The tweet is more about the FRAMING than the actual content
- WWE commentary language on non-WWE events

### 9-10/10: Weapons-Grade Ragebait
- Pure manufactured outrage with zero informational value
- Deliberately misleading or out-of-context content
- Designed EXCLUSIVELY to make people angry-share
- The content wouldn't go viral without the inflammatory framing

## Critical Analysis Rules

1. **ALWAYS analyze the FRAMING, not just the underlying content.** A real event can
   absolutely be ragebait if the framing is designed to provoke rather than inform.
2. **Judge the TWEET, not the account.** Don't give anyone benefit of the doubt or
   extra suspicion based on who they are. Analyze what's in front of you.
3. **Look at the language.** Words like "SMOKED", "DESTROYED", "OWNED", "SLAMMED"
   are huge ragebait indicators — you don't need wrestling commentary to share news.
4. **Check for injected framing.** Would this content provoke outrage on its own, or
   does it only become outrage because of how it's presented?
5. **The "framing vs content" test.** Strip away the caption and look at the raw
   content. If the raw content is benign but the tweet makes it feel like a fight,
   that's ragebait.
6. **The "who benefits" test.** Does the engagement benefit the audience (they learn
   something useful) or is it just engagement for engagement's sake?
7. **Emotional priming is a red flag.** If the tweet tells you how to feel (😂🤣💀)
   before you've even processed the content, that's a manipulation tactic.
