# Ragebait Knowledge Base

## What Is Ragebait?

"Rage bait" — Oxford's 2025 Word of the Year — is online content deliberately designed
to elicit anger or outrage by being frustrating, provocative, or offensive. The creator
benefits from engagement regardless of whether people agree or disagree. The goal is
NOT to inform — it's to make you react.

Ragebait exploits a hardwired human bias. As social psychologist Dr. William Brady
explains: "In our past, this is the kind of content that we really needed to pay
attention to, so we have these biases built into our learning and our attention."
Platforms reward this — comments born from anger are treated as "higher quality
engagement" by algorithms. The poster gets paid. You get manipulated.

## Why It Works (The Psychology)

- Negative content hijacks attention more than positive content — evolutionary survival instinct
- Anger is the most viral emotion — angry people share, comment, and quote-tweet more
- Algorithms amplify outrage because outrage = engagement = revenue
- Both sides engage: one side angry-shares, the other victory-shares — the poster wins either way
- "If we see a cat, we're like 'oh cute' and scroll on. But if we see someone doing
  something obscene, we type in the comments 'this is terrible' — and that comment is
  seen as higher quality engagement by the algorithm." — Andréa Jones

## Key Ragebait Tactics (Analyze the CONTENT, Not the Account)

### 1. Framing & Editorializing
- Taking a neutral event and adding inflammatory framing ("SMOKED", "DESTROYED", "gets OWNED")
- Cherry-picking a moment and presenting it as if it represents something bigger
- Adding commentary that tells you how to feel before you even see the content
- Using words like "just", "breaking", "wow" to manufacture urgency
- Example: A kid says something funny at a space launch. That's benign. But framing
  it as "Kid just SMOKED a CNN reporter" turns a cute moment into a culture war battle.
  That's textbook ragebait — the FRAMING is the manipulation, not the content.

### 2. Culture War Injection
- Taking an apolitical moment and injecting political framing
- Pitting groups against each other (media vs people, left vs right, etc.)
- "Main character" framing — making someone the hero or villain of a narrative
- Using a kid or sympathetic figure as a prop in an ideological argument
- Election-season ragebait: "hyper-focused on 'X is horrible for this reason'" with
  zero policy substance — pure mobilization through outrage

### 3. Outrage Farming / Engagement Farming
- Content designed so that BOTH agreement and disagreement drive engagement
- Removing context that would make the situation seem normal or nuanced
- Screenshots/clips taken out of context to maximize reaction
- Deliberately vague or controversial takes that force people to pick a side
- Creators literally get paid per engagement — some make $150K+/year from hate comments alone

### 4. Emotional Manipulation & Priming
- Asterisk-censoring swear words (f*cking) — makes reader fill in the blank = more engagement
- Using laughing/crying emojis (😂🤣💀) to signal "this is ridiculous" and prime your reaction
- "I can't believe..." or "Imagine..." framing
- Rhetorical questions designed to provoke, not inform
- Telling you how to feel BEFORE you see the content

### 5. Inflammatory Language
- ALL CAPS for emphasis on emotionally charged words
- WWE/sports commentary applied to news or culture ("SMOKED", "DESTROYED",
  "OBLITERATED", "OWNED", "SLAMMED", "CLAPPED BACK")
- Hyperbolic descriptions of mundane events
- Sarcasm and snark designed to belittle rather than analyze
- "Ratio" bait — saying something provocative specifically to get quote-tweeted

### 6. Ragebait Content Formats
- Outrageous food recipes or life hacks (designed to make you comment "that's disgusting")
- "Hot takes" on beloved cultural figures or fandoms
- Political content stripped of nuance, designed to make one side look insane
- Misrepresenting someone's position to make it easier to attack
- Repackaging real news with maximum outrage framing (aggregator behavior)
- "Look what THEY'RE doing now" format

## Rating Calibration

### 1-2/10: Not Ragebait
- Original reporting or genuine personal stories
- Content that informs without trying to provoke a specific emotional reaction
- Nuanced takes that acknowledge complexity
- Sharing something interesting without inflammatory framing
- Actual journalism, even if the topic itself is controversial

### 3-4/10: Light Seasoning
- Slightly editorialized but mostly informational
- Mild framing bias but the core content is real and presented fairly
- A little spicy but not manipulative
- Opinion content that's clearly opinion, not disguised as news

### 5-6/10: Deliberate Buttons Being Pushed
- Clear editorial framing designed to provoke a specific reaction
- Cherry-picked content with noticeable spin
- "Look at this!" energy without full context
- The framing adds more heat than light
- You can feel the engagement optimization at work

### 7-8/10: Industrial-Grade Ragebait
- Inflammatory framing that transforms mundane content into outrage
- Obvious culture war framing on apolitical events
- Key context removed or distorted to maximize emotional reaction
- The tweet is more about the FRAMING than the actual content
- WWE commentary language applied to non-WWE events
- Both sides will engage — that's the point
- You're being played and can feel it

### 9-10/10: Weapons-Grade Ragebait
- Pure manufactured outrage with zero informational value
- Deliberately misleading or out-of-context content
- Designed EXCLUSIVELY to make people angry-share
- The content wouldn't go viral without the inflammatory framing
- Misinformation dressed up as commentary
- Professional engagement farming — someone is getting paid for your anger

## Critical Analysis Rules

1. **ALWAYS analyze the FRAMING, not just the underlying content.** A real event can
   absolutely be ragebait if the framing is designed to provoke rather than inform.
2. **Judge the TWEET, not the account.** Don't give anyone benefit of the doubt or
   extra suspicion based on who they are. Analyze what's in front of you.
3. **Look at the language.** Words like "SMOKED", "DESTROYED", "OWNED", "SLAMMED"
   are huge ragebait indicators — you don't need wrestling commentary to share news.
4. **Check for injected framing.** Would this content provoke outrage on its own, or
   does it only become outrage because of how it's presented?
5. **The "framing vs content" test.** Strip away the caption and look at the raw
   content. If the raw content is benign but the tweet makes it feel like a fight,
   that's ragebait.
6. **The "who benefits" test.** Does the engagement benefit the audience (they learn
   something useful) or is it just engagement for engagement's sake?
7. **Emotional priming is a red flag.** If the tweet tells you how to feel (😂🤣💀)
   before you've even processed the content, that's a manipulation tactic.
8. **The algorithm rewards anger.** Remember: negative comments count as "higher
   quality engagement." If a tweet seems designed to generate angry replies from
   BOTH sides, it's almost certainly ragebait.
9. **Context removal = ragebait signal.** If the tweet only works as outrage because
   context has been stripped away, that's manipulation.
10. **Politically neutral analysis.** Left-wing ragebait and right-wing ragebait
    both get called out equally. Ragebait is ragebait regardless of ideology.
